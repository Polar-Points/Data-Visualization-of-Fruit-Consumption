This is a interactive recreation of Professor Nelson's Expenditure-Income graph, where cumulative income share is shown on the x-axis, and cumulative expenditure of organic fruits share is on the y-axis. As both of these values are market shares, their values are maximally one and minimally zero. The drop down menu allows for users to choose a market region they want to investigate. The home button allows them to return to the original multiline graph.

The 45-degree line is supposed to show what happens in the relationship between consumption and income when each income group spends the exact same amount on a type of organic fruit. The curve lines display the actual relationship between these two variables in a specific market region.

An example of how to interprete the graph: Louieville's expenditure/income line follows the 45-degree line quite closely, which means that in this region, each income group spends relatively the same amount of money on organic fruits. However, Jacksonville's line arcs up and away from the 45-degree line at first, and level out when income increases. This demonstrates that people in the low and middle income brackets actually occupies a bigger share of the total's expenditure on organic fruits than those in the upper income brackets. To put it more simply and crudely, in Jacksonville, the poorer and middle classes spend more on organic fruit than the upper class.
